🛡️ CYBERSECURITY GUIDE FOR EVERYONE

NIS2 has arrived. It does not apply to all — but all should prepare.

📌 Introduction — Why this guide exists
NIS2 applies only to essential and important entities.
However, even micro and small businesses — which are not directly covered — will feel its impact.

This happens because:

NIS2 will raise the security expectations across the entire supply chain  
Large organizations will require minimum security practices from their suppliers.

SMEs remain the easiest target for ransomware  
Attackers choose vulnerability, not size.

Most successful attacks begin in small companies  
A compromised supplier can compromise a critical client.

A minimum level of cybersecurity will become a practical requirement  
Even without legal obligation, the market will demand it.

This guide exists to help any company — even without an IT team — raise its security level in a simple, proportional and sustainable way.

🧭 1. What an SME needs to know (without legal jargon)
✔ NIS2 does not require expensive technology
It requires good practices, discipline and simple processes.

✔ A CISO or dedicated specialist is not necessary
In a microbusiness, that would be unrealistic and unsustainable.

✔ Cybersecurity can be managed by a single person
With accumulated responsibilities and a few hours per month.

✔ The goal is not certification
It is reducing risk, protecting operations and increasing trust.

🧩 2. Essential cybersecurity checklist for SMEs
This checklist represents the “minimum viable” level for any company.

2.1 Governance
[ ] Appoint an internal person responsible for cybersecurity

[ ] Create a simple security policy (5–7 pages)

[ ] Provide annual training for all employees

[ ] Ensure management understands its legal responsibility

2.2 Inventory and assets
[ ] List of computers, servers, applications and suppliers

[ ] Identification of critical systems (ERP, invoicing, email, backups)

[ ] Mapping of external dependencies (cloud, telecom, software)

2.3 Access and identity
🔐 What is MFA?
MFA (Multi‑Factor Authentication) requires two steps to access a system:

password

a second factor (mobile code, authenticator app, biometrics)

Even if someone discovers the password, they cannot log in without the second factor.

Essential measures:
[ ] Enable MFA whenever possible

[ ] Remove old or unused accounts

[ ] Review access rights quarterly

[ ] Create simple password rules

2.4 Backups
[ ] Automated backups

[ ] Test recovery twice a year

[ ] Keep an offline or immutable copy

2.5 Vulnerabilities
[ ] Automatic updates on computers and servers

[ ] Monthly check for outdated software

[ ] Removal of unused applications

2.6 Monitoring and incidents
[ ] Use antivirus/EDR with alerts

[ ] Create a simple incident response plan

[ ] Define emergency contacts

[ ] Record all incidents, even minor ones

2.7 Suppliers (realistic SME version)
Most micro and small companies do not have formal supplier assessment processes — and that is normal.

The goal is simplicity:

[ ] List of critical suppliers (cloud, ERP, IT, telecom)

[ ] Confirmation that they use MFA and backups

[ ] Simple question: “Do you notify clients in case of an incident?”

[ ] Keep an email with this confirmation

This is enough for 99% of SMEs.

👥 3. Cybersecurity roles in a micro/small business
3.1 One internal person with partial responsibility
In a microbusiness, one person is enough.
This can be someone with basic IT knowledge, technical training, or the existing IT lead (if any).

This person should:
report directly to the owner/manager

be independent from the IT team (to avoid conflicts)

dedicate a few hours per month

keep processes simple and updated

3.2 What this person does in practice
Create cybersecurity awareness

Remove old or unnecessary software

Enforce strong passwords and MFA

Prevent passwords written on paper

Keep drivers and systems updated

Check backups

Document access and incidents

Reinforce good practices with the team

It is a practical, disciplined role integrated into daily operations.

3.3 External support (only when necessary)
There is no need to hire a SOC, continuous audits or permanent consulting.

The SME should simply identify an external partner for:

major incidents

occasional audits

specific consulting

No monthly fees.
No dependency.
No complexity.

⭐ Conclusion — A journey is made of steps
Cybersecurity is not built in a day.
It does not require heavy investments, dedicated teams or complex processes.
It requires first steps, consistency and the willingness to improve over time.

This guide represents exactly that:
the first steps any micro or small business can take, even with limited resources, without an IT team, and while accumulating responsibilities.

Over time, it will be possible to evolve, strengthen controls, improve processes and, if needed, seek external support.
But it is not necessary to do everything now.
The important thing is to begin.

Security is never too much.
Even if NIS2 does not directly apply to micro or small companies, the trend is clear: to work with the State, large organizations or regulated sectors, a minimum level of cybersecurity will increasingly be required from suppliers.

Preparing now — with simple, proportional and sustainable measures — reduces risks, increases client trust and positions any company to meet future market demands.

Cybersecurity is no longer a differentiator.
It is an essential requirement to grow, compete and ensure continuity in an increasingly demanding digital environment.
